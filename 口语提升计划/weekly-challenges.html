🎯 8-Week Speaking Challenges

One speaking task per week. Record yourself, listen back, and compare to last week. This is the most direct path to real fluency — because fluency is built by speaking, not by studying.

🎙
Record Every Time
Use your phone's Voice Memo app. Recording creates accountability and lets you hear your actual progress.
👂
Listen Back Critically
Listen for: fluency, filler words (um, uh), grammar issues, and whether you sound natural. Pick one thing to improve.
📈
Compare Week to Week
Keep all your recordings. The difference between Week 1 and Week 8 will be your best motivation to keep going.

W1
Self Introduction
Speak for 90 seconds about who you are and what you do
Foundation
▼
🎯 Goal: Introduce yourself naturally in English for 90 seconds. Cover: name, role, what your team does, what you're working on, and one professional achievement or challenge you're proud of.
Your Speaking Prompt
🎙 Say this out loud (record on your phone):
Imagine you're joining a new international project and meeting the team for the first time. Introduce yourself covering:
  • Your name and current role
  • What your team or product does (in simple language)
  • What you're currently working on
  • One thing you're good at or proud of professionally
  • One thing you're looking forward to in this project
Target: 90 seconds. Don't script it word-for-word — speak naturally from notes.
📱 Recording Instructions
1. Open Voice Memos (iOS) or Recorder (Android)
2. Say the prompt cold — don't prepare too much
3. Listen back immediately. Note one thing that was good, one to improve
4. Record a second version with that improvement
5. Save both. Label them "W1-take1" and "W1-take2"
Self-Check — After Recording
Notes (what went well / what to improve)
W2
Describe Your Work Day
Explain what you do in simple English for a non-technical listener
Foundation
▼
🎯 Goal: Describe a typical work day or work week in 2 minutes, using simple English that a non-technical friend would understand. Avoid jargon — if you must use a technical term, explain it briefly.
🎙 Say this out loud:
Imagine your friend who doesn't work in tech asks: "So what exactly do you do at work?" Describe your typical day covering:
  • What your job is and what your team is responsible for
  • What a typical morning looks like
  • The types of problems you solve
  • What "a good day at work" looks and feels like for you
Key challenge: Explain one technical concept (API, sprint, deployment, etc.) in simple language. No jargon without explanation.
📱 Recording & Review
Record → Listen back → Compare to Week 1 recording. Are you more fluent? Fewer pauses? More natural vocabulary? Write one specific improvement you notice.

W3
The Daily Standup
Master Yesterday / Today / Blocker — the most used format in tech
Team
▼
🎯 Goal: Deliver a complete, natural standup update in under 90 seconds. No script — speak from real work. Then do it again three times this week in your actual standup meetings.
🎙 The Format — practice until automatic:
  • Yesterday: "Yesterday I worked on / finished / was reviewing…"
  • Today: "Today I'm planning to / going to focus on…"
  • Blocker: "I don't have any blockers right now." / "I'm blocked on [issue] — I might need [name]'s help with that."

Practice variations — say each one aloud:
  • "Yesterday I finished the API integration. Today I'll start on the unit tests. No blockers."
  • "Yesterday I was in code review most of the day. Today I'm picking up the login bug — it should be done by end of day. I'm waiting on the design spec, so I might be blocked later."
  • "Yesterday I couldn't make much progress because of an environment issue. Today I'll pair with Tom to sort that out first. That's my main blocker right now."
📱 This Week's Goal
Speak your standup aloud before each real meeting — so you've already said the words before you have to say them in front of others. Record at least one real or practice standup this week.

W4
Explain a Technical Concept
Make complex ideas simple — a core skill for senior engineers
Team
▼
🎯 Goal: Choose one technical concept from your daily work. Explain it clearly in 2 minutes to an imaginary non-technical manager or client. No jargon without immediate explanation.
🎙 Choose one topic and explain it:
Pick any concept you actually use: API, Git branches, unit testing, a sprint, a database, a deployment pipeline, microservices, a pull request, or load balancing.

Structure your explanation:
  • What it is: "So [concept] is basically…"
  • Analogy: "Think of it like [everyday thing]…"
  • Why it matters: "The reason we care about this is…"
  • Simple example: "For instance, when we [real scenario]…"
The "analogy" step is the hardest and most important. If you can explain a technical concept using a real-world comparison, you've truly understood it.
📱 Bonus Challenge
After recording, actually explain this concept to a real non-technical colleague, family member, or friend this week. Notice what they understand immediately and what needs more explanation.

W5
Handle an Unexpected Question
The skill that separates confident speakers from prepared ones
Client
▼
🎯 Goal: Practice responding to unexpected questions naturally — using buying-time phrases, structuring your answer, and not freezing under pressure.
🎙 Practice with these questions (answer each one — don't prepare):
Ask a friend or colleague to read these to you, one at a time. Answer immediately. Or read each question, look away, and answer aloud.
  • "Why is this feature taking longer than expected?"
  • "Can you explain the architecture decision you made last week?"
  • "What would happen if we launched this without fully testing it?"
  • "How would you handle a situation where two teammates disagree?"
  • "What's the biggest technical risk in the current project?"
  • "If you had to recommend one improvement to our team's process, what would it be?"
Key skill: Start with a buying-time phrase. Then structure with "Well, there are a couple of things to consider here…"
🛠 Buying-Time Phrases to Practice
• "That's a good question. Let me think about that for a second."
• "There are a few things to consider here…"
• "My initial reaction is… but let me also consider…"
• "I'd want to check the details on that — but off the top of my head…"

W6
Disagree Politely & Persuade
Push back without damaging relationships — advanced professional skill
Client
▼
🎯 Goal: Practice saying the same disagreement 3 different ways — from very gentle to more direct. Real professional fluency means being able to calibrate your tone to the situation.
🎙 Scenario: Your manager suggests launching next week. You think it's too early.
Practice saying your disagreement in 3 levels:
  • Gentle (with a client or senior executive):
    "I completely understand the urgency, and I want to support that. I do want to flag one concern about the timeline — the testing isn't complete yet, and I'd hate for that to cause issues after launch. Could we explore a partial release, or a short delay on just one feature?"
  • Direct (with your manager):
    "I'd push back on that timeline a bit. We still have outstanding bugs, and I think launching now carries real risk. My suggestion would be to delay by one week — it gives us time to fix the critical issues without significantly impacting the overall schedule."
  • Firm (when it's a safety or quality issue):
    "I have to be honest — I don't think we're ready. The core feature has a bug that will affect 30% of users. I'd recommend we delay until that's resolved. I know that's not what we want to hear, but I think it's the right call."
Now create your own version for a real situation you've faced at work. Record all three levels.

W7
Project Status Update
3-minute update for a client or senior stakeholder
Client
▼
🎯 Goal: Give a complete, confident 3-minute project status update to an imaginary client or executive — covering progress, risks, and next steps without needing prompts.
🎙 Structure your update (use a real project you're working on):
  • Overall status: "The project is on track / slightly behind / ahead of schedule. Here's a quick summary…"
  • What's been completed: "Since our last update, we've completed / delivered / finished…"
  • What's in progress: "We're currently working on… which we expect to finish by…"
  • Risks or concerns: "There is one thing I want to flag — [issue]. The potential impact is [X], and our plan to address it is [Y]."
  • Next steps: "Over the next [timeframe], we'll be focusing on… The next milestone is [date]."
  • Closing: "Happy to go deeper on any of these areas — what would be most useful?"
Practice this twice: once without timing, then once with a timer. 3 minutes is about 400 words spoken naturally.

W8
Full Mock Meeting
10-minute AI-simulated meeting — test everything you've learned
Advanced
▼
🎯 Goal: Run a 10-minute mock meeting using all skills from the 8 weeks. Use an AI assistant to simulate the other participants. Record the full session.
🎙 Setup — copy this prompt into ChatGPT or Claude:
"Please simulate a project update meeting. You play the role of a client (or manager) who wants a project update. Ask me about progress, raise one concern about the timeline, and ask one unexpected question. I'll play the developer giving the update. Let's go — start by opening the meeting."

During the meeting, try to use:
  • An opening phrase from Core 30
  • At least one buying-time phrase
  • At least one polite pushback phrase
  • A clear summary at the end with next steps
After the meeting, listen to your recording and count how many Core 30 phrases you used naturally. This is your fluency benchmark.
📊 Compare to Week 1
Listen to your Week 1 self-introduction recording. Then listen to this Week 8 mock meeting. The difference is your 8-week progress. Write down 3 specific improvements you can hear.
Final Self-Assessment
Final Reflection
🔁 After Week 8: Repeat the cycle. Choose more challenging topics. The standup becomes a real client meeting. The self-introduction becomes a conference talk intro. The project update becomes a board-level summary. Fluency is built through cycles of challenge → practice → reflection → new challenge — not through a single course.
