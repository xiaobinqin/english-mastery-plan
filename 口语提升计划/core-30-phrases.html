Opening & Starting
Kick off meetings, calls, and conversations confidently
0/5 mastered
Let me kick things off by… / I'd like to start by…
📍 Use: Opening any meeting or presentation — natural, confident
💬 "Let me kick things off by giving a quick status update." / "I'd like to start by reviewing what we finished last week."
✓ Mastered
Thanks for joining. Today we'll cover…
📍 Use: Setting the agenda at the start of a meeting
💬 "Thanks for joining. Today we'll cover the sprint review and then discuss the API issue." — Clear, professional, sets expectations.
✓ Mastered
Do you have a few minutes? I have a quick question.
📍 Use: Approaching a colleague informally — respectful of their time
💬 This phrase shows respect for the other person's time. Much better than just starting to talk. The "quick" signals you won't take long.
✓ Mastered
I just wanted to quickly check in on…
📍 Use: Following up on a task, project, or previous conversation
💬 "I just wanted to quickly check in on the deployment — are we still on track for Friday?" — Friendly, not aggressive.
✓ Mastered
Before we get started, does anyone have anything urgent to add?
📍 Use: Opening a structured meeting — shows leadership and inclusivity
💬 This one phrase makes you sound like a confident meeting facilitator. Practice it until it feels completely natural.
✓ Mastered
💬
Expressing Your Opinion
Share your view clearly — not too aggressive, not too passive
0/5 mastered
My take on this is… / From my perspective…
📍 Use: Sharing your opinion without sounding too absolute
💬 "My take on this is that we should fix the bug before releasing." — "My take" sounds natural and confident in English-speaking teams. Avoid "I think" alone, which can sound uncertain.
✓ Mastered
I'd lean towards [option], because…
📍 Use: Recommending an option when there are multiple choices
💬 "I'd lean towards the microservices approach, because it gives us more flexibility down the road." — "lean towards" is a very natural native phrase that signals preference without being rigid.
✓ Mastered
Just to push back a little — have we considered…?
📍 Use: Politely disagreeing or raising an alternative — essential in tech teams
💬 "Just to push back a little — have we considered the performance impact of that approach?" — "push back" is a standard professional phrase. It signals healthy debate, not conflict.
✓ Mastered
I see your point, but I think we also need to consider…
📍 Use: Acknowledging someone's idea before offering a different view
💬 Always acknowledge before disagreeing — this keeps the conversation collaborative. "I see your point, but I think we also need to consider the timeline risk."
✓ Mastered
That's a good point. I hadn't thought of it that way.
📍 Use: Genuinely accepting someone else's idea — builds trust and rapport
💬 Don't overuse this, but when it's genuine, it's very powerful for building team relationships. Native speakers say this a lot.
✓ Mastered

Buying Time & Asking for Clarity
Never freeze — use these to control pace and get what you need
0/5 mastered
Sorry, could you say that again? / Could you repeat that?
📍 Use: When you miss something — never pretend you understood
💬 This is one of the most important phrases to say naturally. Practice it until it comes out automatically. Many learners pretend they understood — this leads to bigger problems later.
✓ Mastered
Just to make sure I understand — you're saying that…?
📍 Use: Confirming your understanding, especially for complex requests
💬 This phrase does two things: confirms you understood, AND shows you were listening carefully. "Just to make sure I understand — you're saying the deadline moved to next Thursday?"
✓ Mastered
That's a good question. Let me think about that for a second.
📍 Use: Buying 3–5 seconds of thinking time without sounding lost
💬 This is your "pause button." Say it, then think. Never go silent for more than 2 seconds without saying something — silence reads as confusion. This phrase reads as thoughtfulness.
✓ Mastered
I'm not 100% sure on that. Let me get back to you.
📍 Use: When you don't know the answer — far better than guessing wrong
💬 This builds trust. In a professional environment, saying "I don't know but I'll find out" is far more respected than guessing. Always follow up when you say this.
✓ Mastered
Could you give me an example? / Can you walk me through that?
📍 Use: When an explanation is too abstract — ask for concrete specifics
💬 Asking for examples is a sign of engagement, not weakness. "Can you walk me through that?" is especially useful for technical explanations.
✓ Mastered

Keeping Conversation Going
Stay engaged, show interest, ask naturally
0/5 mastered
What do you think about…? / How do you feel about…?
📍 Use: Inviting someone's opinion — opens dialogue naturally
💬 "What do you think about moving the deployment to Monday?" — This is essential for inclusive meetings. Use it often with quieter team members.
✓ Mastered
That's interesting — tell me more.
📍 Use: Showing genuine interest and encouraging someone to continue
💬 Simple and powerful. This phrase signals that you're engaged and that what they said matters. Use in 1-on-1s, team check-ins, client calls.
✓ Mastered
So what you're saying is… (rephrase their point)
📍 Use: Active listening — paraphrase before responding to confirm understanding
💬 This is the single most powerful listening technique. "So what you're saying is the client wants the new feature before the bug fixes?" — It prevents misunderstandings and shows respect.
✓ Mastered
I've actually had a similar experience. When I…
📍 Use: Connecting your own experience to theirs — builds conversation naturally
💬 Sharing a relevant story is one of the best conversation techniques. Keep it short — 2–3 sentences max — then return to them: "Has it been similar for you?"
✓ Mastered
Heads up — [important information coming]
📍 Use: Warning someone about something upcoming — very natural in English teams
💬 "Heads up — I'll be out of office Friday, so can we move the review to Thursday?" — "Heads up" is casual and friendly. Use in Slack, email, or spoken. Very common in tech teams.
✓ Mastered
✅
Wrapping Up & Summarizing
End meetings and conversations with clarity and professionalism
0/5 mastered
So just to summarize what we agreed…
📍 Use: End of any meeting — summarizing decisions and next steps
💬 Always summarize at the end of a meeting. This one habit will make you look extremely professional. "So just to summarize — we agreed to delay the launch by one week and Tom will update the client."
✓ Mastered
The next step would be for [name] to… by [date].
📍 Use: Assigning action items clearly at the end of a meeting
💬 "The next step would be for Sarah to share the updated spec by Wednesday." — This makes responsibilities clear without sounding pushy. Essential for project management.
✓ Mastered
Is there anything else you'd like to cover?
📍 Use: Before officially ending a meeting — shows thoroughness
💬 This small question prevents the "oh wait, one more thing" that happens after meetings. Say it before closing, not after you've said goodbye.
✓ Mastered
Let's plan to follow up on this by [day].
📍 Use: Keeping momentum — ensuring unresolved issues get addressed
💬 "Let's plan to follow up on this by Thursday — does that work for everyone?" Always set a specific day, not "soon" or "next week."
✓ Mastered
Thanks, this was really helpful. / Good meeting, everyone.
📍 Use: Closing warmly — a small thing that makes a real difference to team culture
💬 Endings matter. "Thanks, this was really helpful" leaves people feeling the time was well spent. It's a small habit with a large impact on how others perceive you.
✓ Mastered
☀️
Daily Small Talk
Build rapport — fluency lives in informal conversation too
0/5 mastered
How's your week going so far? / How are things on your end?
📍 Use: Starting any casual conversation — more natural than "How are you?"
💬 "How are you?" can feel formal. "How's your week going?" is warmer and invites a real answer. "How are things on your end?" is great for remote team check-ins.
✓ Mastered
Did you get a chance to look at [the PR / the doc / the issue]?
📍 Use: Following up without sounding demanding — natural in tech teams
💬 "Did you get a chance to…" is softer than "Did you review…?" It acknowledges the other person is busy and gives them room to say not yet without feeling bad.
✓ Mastered
No worries, it happens. / Don't worry about it.
📍 Use: When someone apologizes — responding with warmth, not just "ok"
💬 When someone says sorry, responding with "No worries, it happens" instead of just "ok" or silence makes you sound much warmer and more natural. Very common in English-speaking workplaces.
✓ Mastered
Great work on [task/project], by the way.
📍 Use: Giving informal praise — builds team culture and rapport
💬 "Great work on the demo yesterday, by the way — the client was really impressed." — Small, specific, genuine praise is one of the most powerful things you can say. "By the way" keeps it casual.
✓ Mastered
Sounds good to me. / Works for me. / I'm on board.
📍 Use: Agreeing — these are much more natural than "OK" or "Yes"
💬 Native speakers rarely say just "yes" or "ok" to agree. "Sounds good," "Works for me," and "I'm on board" are the natural alternatives. Pick one and make it your default.
✓ Mastered
