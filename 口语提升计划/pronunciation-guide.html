Pronunciation Training Guide

6 Units · Targeted for Chinese-English Speakers · Minimal Pairs · Sentence Drills · Software Context Examples

Why Pronunciation Matters More Than Vocabulary

You can know 10,000 English words perfectly, but if your pronunciation makes people strain to understand you, the communication breaks down. The good news: you don't need a perfect accent. You need clear, intelligible speech. These 6 units address the specific sounds that Chinese-English speakers most commonly mispronounce, causing real-world misunderstandings in meetings.

Unit 1 — The /θ/ and /ð/ Sounds (th)

⚠️ Why this matters: Chinese has no /th/ sound. Speakers typically replace it with /s/, /z/, /d/, or /t/. "Think" sounds like "sink", "the" sounds like "de", "three" like "slee". In meetings: "the team" → "de team", "three weeks" → "tree weeks". Clients and colleagues notice this.
🫦 How to produce /θ/ (voiceless th — think, three, through):
Place the tip of your tongue lightly between your upper and lower teeth. Push air out gently. Your tongue tip should barely be visible. Do NOT make a sound in your throat — just air. It's a "breathy" sound.

🫦 How to produce /ð/ (voiced th — the, this, that, they, there):
Same tongue position, but now add voice — vibrate your vocal cords. If you put your hand on your throat, you should feel vibration. This is the most common th sound in English.

Unit 2 — /v/ vs /w/ and /r/ vs /l/

⚠️ Why this matters: /v/ and /w/ are two completely different sounds but often confused. "Version" becomes "wersion", "value" becomes "walue". For /r/ and /l/ — "release" vs "rerese", "client" vs "cient". In tech context: "version", "value", "release", "load", "review" all have these sounds.
🫦 /v/ (as in version, value, verify): Upper front teeth rest gently on your lower lip. Blow air through the gap while vibrating your vocal cords. Your lips should NOT come together. Feel the gentle friction between teeth and lip.

🫦 /w/ (as in work, what, we): Round your lips into a tight circle (like you're about to whistle or say "oo"). Then quickly open them as you say the word. No teeth involved.

🫦 /r/ (as in release, review, requirements): Curl the tip of your tongue back and up — it should NOT touch anything. Round your lips slightly. It's a smooth sound, never trilled in American/British English.

🫦 /l/ (as in load, login, level): Touch the tip of your tongue to the ridge just behind your upper front teeth. Air flows around the sides of the tongue.

Unit 3 — Word-Final Consonants (Endings That Disappear)

⚠️ Why this matters: Chinese syllables almost always end in a vowel sound. So Chinese-English speakers frequently drop or weaken word-final consonants. "Fixed" sounds like "fixe", "blocked" like "blocke", "tests" like "tes". This completely changes meaning: "build" ≠ "built" ≠ "builds". Crucial for communicating past/present/future and plurals.

The Most Commonly Dropped Endings in Software Context

EndingCommon wordsHow to practice
-ed /d/ or /t/fixed, blocked, merged, deployed, tested, failed, pushed, reviewedAfter a voiced sound → /d/. After unvoiced → /t/. Feel your tongue tip tap the roof of your mouth then STOP. Don't add a vowel: "fixed" NOT "fix-ed".
-s / -esbugs, tests, builds, commits, services, branches, releasesAfter a voiced sound → /z/. After unvoiced → /s/. After s/z/sh → /ɪz/. Always complete the final sound — don't let it disappear.
-trequest, commit, rollout, sprint, input, output, submit, promptTouch tongue to upper ridge and create a tiny "pop". Don't let it become a glottal stop or disappear.
-kcheck, stack, track, block, mock, click, hook, workThe back of your tongue touches the soft palate and releases with a tiny pop. "Check" NOT "cheh".
-pstop, step, loop, group, app, wrap, mapLips come together and release — a tiny burst of air. "Stop" NOT "stoh".

3Please check the stack trace and submit a bug report.
4The request was blocked because the token had expired.
5I've reviewed the specs and tracked down the root cause.

Unit 4 — Word Stress (Which Syllable Gets the Beat)

⚠️ Why this matters: English is a stress-timed language — the wrong syllable stress makes words unrecognizable even if every sound is correct. "DEvelop" vs "deLOP" — native speakers might not recognize the word. In tech: "REcord" (noun) vs "reCORD" (verb) is a real distinction that changes meaning.

The Golden Rule: Stressed syllables are LOUDER, LONGER, and HIGHER in pitch

Unit 5 — Sentence Stress (What to Emphasize in Meetings)

⚠️ Why this matters: In English sentences, only the most important words receive stress — verbs, nouns, adjectives. Articles, prepositions, and auxiliaries ("a, the, is, can, will") are typically reduced and unstressed. Chinese speakers often stress every word equally, which sounds robotic and makes it harder for listeners to identify the key information.

Rule: Stress the CONTENT words. Reduce the FUNCTION words.

🔁 Meeting Sentence Drills (underlined = stressed)

Stressed words are shown in bold underlined. Say the unstressed words quickly and lightly.

1We need to finish the API by Friday.
2The client wants a demo on Thursday morning.
3I've identified the root cause — it's a race condition.
4We're on track, but there's a risk around the database migration.
5The tests are passing, but the performance is still below the target.

Changing Meaning by Changing Stress

The same sentence means different things depending on which word you stress:

SentenceMeaning
I didn't say the deadline changed.Someone else said it, not me.
I didn't say the deadline changed.I implied it, but didn't explicitly say it.
I didn't say the deadline changed.Something else changed, not the deadline.
I didn't say the deadline changed.I said it might change, not that it definitely did.

Unit 6 — Connected Speech (Sounding Natural, Not Word-by-Word)

⚠️ Why this matters: Native speakers never say words one-by-one in isolation. They link sounds together in streams. "Did you eat?" becomes "Didja eat?" — this is not lazy pronunciation; it's how fluent English sounds. When you understand these patterns, you'll understand native speakers much better AND sound more natural yourself.

Pattern 1: Linking (consonant + vowel connect)

When a word ends in a consonant and the next word starts with a vowel, they link together.

1Written: "check it" → Spoken: "check_it" (checki-t)
2Written: "log out" → Spoken: "log_out" (logout)
3Written: "talk about it" → Spoken: "talk_about_it" (talka-bou-dit)
4Written: "work on it" → Spoken: "work_on_it" (worko-nit)
5Written: "ship it" → Spoken: "ship_it" (shippit)

Pattern 2: Reduction (unstressed words get shorter)

Written formSpoken formExample
